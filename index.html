The data provided gives us a glimpse, into the restaurant inspection results for types of cuisine in New York City spanning from 2021 to 2024. The grades. A, B and C. Signify the degree of adherence to health regulations. Grade A indicates compliance with no critical violations Grade B suggests moderate compliance while Grade C points out significant regulatory infractions.

In terms of cuisine categories American eateries consistently upheld compliance levels with Grade A percentages exceeding 80% until a noticeable decline to 60.4% in 2024. This decrease signifies a drop in adherence accompanied by corresponding rises in Grades B and C. On the contrary Chinese cuisine started off strong with 79.7% attaining an A grade in 2021 but witnessed a decline over the years to reach 42.5% by 2024. This indicates a decrease in adherence or stricter enforcement of health regulations.

Mexican cuisine saw fluctuating rates of compliance beginning at a 82.73% Grade A in 2021 before dropping to 47.68% by 2024. This decline was coupled with increases in Grades B and C suggesting emerging challenges regarding health standards or potential shifts in inspection rigor.

Similarly Caribbean cuisine followed this declining trend, in compliance levels – starting at a 74% achieving Grade A in 2021 but dwindling to 44.1% by the year 2024. Both Grade B and C experienced an uptick indicating a trend of decreasing adherence or heightened regulatory oversight.

Japanese cuisine, which began strongly in 2021 with 87.71% receiving Grade A also exhibited a decline, over time dropping to 40.73% by 2024. Notably the rise in grades was less drastic compared to types of cuisine hinting at a more steady compliance in comparison.

In general the data suggests a pattern of diminishing compliance across all styles with an increasing number of eateries receiving lower grades as the years went by. This could signify either a drop in standards, at restaurants or stricter health inspection criteria enforced by authorities.
